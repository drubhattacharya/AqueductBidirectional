What to look for in a health system's community need documentation
✓ Strong CHNA indicators

Published CHNA within 3 years; transportation explicitly named as a barrier; LMI + disability + minority populations documented; quantified disparity data (%, n); county or MSA-level specificity.

⚠ Ask for clarification if...

CHNA is older than 3 years; transportation is cited generally but not quantified; no population-level data on who lacks access; NEMT program not linked to specific CHNA priority.

✗ Red flags

No CHNA citation at all; SDOH narrative is aspirational without data; health system cannot identify the specific populations the program will serve; no Z75.3 or equivalent documentation plan.

Due diligence checklist for the ROI model:
✓ Layer 0: Ask for the break-even trip cost calculation. A program is FFS-positive if trip cost < (revenue per visit − variable cost). Request vendor quotes to verify trip cost assumptions.
✓ Layer 2: Ask for current CCM/TCM enrollment numbers and EHR capability. Many small practices cannot bill CCM without care management infrastructure — this is an implementation risk.
✓ Layer 3: Treat VBC earn-backs as upside, not base case. No study validates NEMT-to-quality-measure attribution end-to-end. Ask for current MIPS scores and what the marginal improvement is worth in dollars.
✓ Layer 4: The HR layer is conservative and directionally correct. Ask for current scheduler FTE count and estimated no-show rework burden. The $200/no-show figure is widely cited across MGMA and Advisory Board research.

Not all funders are the same. Neither are their requirements.

Each funder type operates under different legal structures, has different decision timelines, and requires fundamentally different documentation. This step maps your CHNA priorities to the right vehicles — and tells you exactly what each funder needs.

Understanding where NEMT sits in your funding framework — CRA exam, grantmaking strategy, ESG disclosure, or philanthropy portfolio — determines what due diligence standards apply and what outcomes you should require.

Active regulatory framework 12 CFR 25.23 (OCC)
How CRA credit works for NEMT programs

Under the Community Reinvestment Act, banks earn CRA credit for community development activities that benefit LMI individuals or geographies in their assessment area. A hospital-run NEMT program serving documented LMI patients can qualify as a community development service, loan, or grant — making it one of the most defensible CRA activities available for banks operating in the same MSA as the health system.

Assessment area confirmation

CRA credit requires the activity to benefit the bank's CRA assessment area. The health system's service area must overlap with the bank's defined assessment area (typically the MSA in which the bank has branches). Patients served must be documented LMI individuals (≤80% of area median income) or residents of LMI census tracts.

• The local transportation safe harbor (42 CFR § 1001.952(bb)) protects the health system from anti-kickback exposure — remove that objection proactively

The 5% payout rule (IRC § 4942): Private non-operating foundations must distribute at least 5% of the fair market value of their noncharitable-use assets each year as qualifying distributions. This mandatory payout creates a reliable annual pool of capital seeking deployment. Both grants and PRIs count toward the 5% requirement. Noncompliance triggers a 30% excise tax on the shortfall. Community foundations — DAFs, competitive grants, and designated funds

Community foundations are public charities (IRC § 509(a)(1)) that manage multiple charitable funds within a defined geography. They offer three distinct mechanisms for NEMT funding, each with different decision timelines, documentation requirements, and strategic advantages.

Mechanism 1
Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) Recommendation
How: A DAF donor recommends a grant to your NEMT program; community foundation executes
Speed: Days to weeks once donor is aligned
Size: $5K–$500K+ (donor-dependent)
Key insight: DAF donors receive immediate deduction at time of contribution to the DAF, not at grant time — they're often looking for impactful deployment of existing charitable capital
Mechanism 2
Competitive Grant (Pooled Endowment)
How: Foundation issues RFP in priority areas; staff-directed grantmaking process
Speed: 3–9 months (RFP → decision → disbursement)
Size: $10K–$100K typical
Key insight: Many community foundations have now designated health equity or SDOH as grantmaking priorities — NEMT is a strong fit. Find the health equity program officer, not the general grants team.
Mechanism 3
Designated or Field-of-Interest Fund
How: A donor establishes a fund restricted to health access or transportation — grants go to qualifying organizations automatically
Speed: Once eligible, faster than competitive
Size: Varies
Key insight: These often go un-found. Call the community foundation's program staff and ask directly: "Do you have any designated funds in health access, SDOH, or transportation?"
Strategic note — DAF cultivation: DAF assets in the U.S. exceeded $250 billion as of 2023 (NPT Donor-Advised Fund Report). Individual DAF donors are often local business leaders or philanthropists with preexisting community relationships. The community foundation can often facilitate introductions. A compelling NEMT narrative — with quantified community need, targeted program design, and an honest ROI — is more persuasive to DAF donors than to institutional grantmakers, because DAF donors respond to impact stories, not compliance frameworks.
Corporate direct giving vs. corporate foundations — not the same thing

This distinction is commonly misunderstood. A corporate foundation is a separate legal entity (a private foundation) subject to IRS private foundation rules, 5% payout, and Form 990-PF disclosure. Direct corporate giving is a business function — not a separate legal entity — with different tax treatment, different constraints, and different decision-makers.

Sponsorship structure note: A sponsorship is a business transaction where a corporation provides financial support in exchange for branding or marketing benefits. Unlike grants, sponsorships may be deducted as a business expense (not limited to the 10% charitable cap) and are documented through a sponsorship agreement rather than a grant agreement. For NEMT, this could mean a local corporation's name on NEMT vehicles, program naming rights ("XYZ Company Community Health Ride"), or visibility in patient-facing materials. The value to the corporation is brand exposure; the value to the health system is flexible, unrestricted revenue.
How to approach a corporate giving conversation
1. Match to the company's stated community priorities.

Review the company's most recent CSR or sustainability report. Look for health, equity, or community access language. Map your NEMT program to those stated priorities — not to NEMT's merits in the abstract.

2. Lead with the community benefit, not the tax deduction.

Corporate community affairs teams are evaluated on community impact, not tax efficiency. Present the CHNA data, the population served, and the measurable outcome metrics. The ROI model is secondary.

3. If it's a sponsorship, involve the marketing team.

Sponsorships are marketing decisions, not philanthropy decisions. The CSR team may not have authority to approve a sponsorship. Ask directly which team handles branded community partnerships.

⚠ Emerging Pathway — No Documented Precedents
Employer coalitions: theoretically grounded, not yet proven in practice

This pathway is included because the theoretical business case is compelling, major employer coalitions have explicitly endorsed medical transportation as a priority, and value-based care contracts between employers and health systems are creating the infrastructure for such arrangements. But after exhaustive research, no documented case was found of an employer coalition directly funding a hospital-based NEMT program. Platform users should treat this as an emerging opportunity, not an established practice.

The business case at each link
• $225.8B/yr — Annual cost of worker illness-related absenteeism (CDC Foundation)
• 2.4× — More likely to miss 6+ workdays if unmet basic needs (transport, food, housing) — Marsh McLennan Agency
• 3.6M Americans miss medical care annually due to transportation barriers — many are in your employer's workforce
• NEBGH (6M lives) explicitly recommends "offering transportation or reimbursement for transit/rideshare costs for health care appointments" in 2022 SDOH guide
Why it hasn't happened yet
• Employer SDOH ROI evidence is thin — no transportation-specific ROI study for commercially insured populations
• ERISA fiduciary duties constrain employer health plan spending to "benefits" — NEMT for non-employees is harder to justify
• Attribution problem: employers don't easily measure whether their NEMT investment reduces their plan costs
• Healthcare Dive (2020): "ROI of the service is less clear in a healthier and wealthier [commercial] population"
The most credible pathway forward
• VBC employer-health system contracts (like GM–Henry Ford "Connected Care") where shared savings fund SDOH programs
• Health system as its own employer: NEMT as employee benefit for LMI workforce (hospital itself funds)
• Anchor institution strategy: NEMT positioned as community workforce investment, not healthcare benefit
• Pilot with measurable outcomes to generate the precedent case that currently doesn't exist
Platform guidance: Do not include employer coalition funding in your core funding ask or primary ROI model. The evidence base cannot yet support it. Instead: (1) explore whether your institution can fund NEMT as an employee benefit for its own LMI workforce, (2) if you have an existing VBC contract with a large self-insured employer, explore whether SDOH investment is explicitly within the shared savings framework, (3) document the outcomes from other funding sources first — the employer coalition opportunity will need a proof case that currently doesn't exist.

04

Every funding relationship requires a defensible legal framework.

Four overlapping legal structures govern NEMT program funding. A compliant program must satisfy all that apply simultaneously. This step walks through each one — with the conditions that must be met, not just the principles.

Local transportation safe harbor — 42 CFR § 1001.952(bb)

The OIG established the local transportation safe harbor in the 2016 Final Rule (81 FR 88368), amended in 2020 (85 FR 77684). It protects transportation arrangements that might otherwise implicate the Anti-Kickback Statute (AKS) — provided all of the following conditions are met:

✓
Written, uniformly applied policy — not based on Medicare/Medicaid status. The policy must apply equally to all patients who meet the eligibility criteria (e.g., LMI, transport barrier, prior no-show).
✓
No volume-or-value linkage — transportation cannot be conditioned on or tied to the volume or value of referrals. The program must be a genuine community benefit, not a patient acquisition tool.
✓
No air, luxury, or ambulance transport — rideshare services (Uber Health, Lyft Healthcare) are explicitly permitted. Standard taxi and van services qualify.
✓
No public advertising of the benefit — the transportation benefit cannot be advertised to the general public as a means of attracting patients. Internal patient notification (letter, care manager call) is permitted.
✓
Established patients only — transportation is for patients who have had at least one prior visit with the healthcare entity. New patient attraction is not protected.
✓
Distance limits — ≤25 miles in urban areas; ≤75 miles in rural areas (expanded from 50 miles in 2020). No distance limit for discharge transportation (the 2020 discharge exception applies to inpatient-to-home transport).
✓
Transportation only for medically necessary services — rides must be to/from covered healthcare services, not for personal errands or social activities.
✓
Entity bears all costs — transportation cost cannot be shifted to federal health programs (Medicaid, Medicare) if the arrangement is within the safe harbor.
Patient engagement safe harbor (42 CFR § 1001.952(hh)) — an alternative pathway:

Permits VBE participants to furnish transportation as an in-kind patient engagement benefit, subject to a $500 annual per-patient cap. This safe harbor applies within value-based enterprise arrangements and has no distance limits. Neither safe harbor is subject to the Inflation Reduction Act moratorium on drug rebate provisions.

Medicare Advantage transportation benefits — post-VBID 2026 framework
VBID Model terminated: The CMS Value-Based Insurance Design (VBID) Model ended at the close of 2025. Plans that had relied on VBID authority for supplemental benefit flexibility must now operate under the standard MA supplemental benefit framework.

Under the current framework (CY2026 Final Rule, CMS-4208-F), MA plans may offer NEMT through two pathways:

Primarily health-related supplemental benefit

Any MA plan may offer transportation for medical needs as a primarily health-related supplemental benefit under 42 CFR § 422.102. Must be primarily health-related (not social). Available to all enrollees (no special eligibility threshold). In 2026: 24% of individual MA plans offer transportation for medical needs.

SSBCI — Special Supplemental Benefits for the Chronically Ill

Under § 422.102(f) (authorized by the Bipartisan Budget Act of 2018), plans may offer transportation for non-medical needs to chronically ill enrollees. Requires: enrollee meets chronic illness definition, benefit is non-primarily health-related, and plan documents a reasonable expectation of health maintenance. In 2026: 67% of SNPs offer this. Transportation is NOT on the CY2026 non-allowable SSBCI list.

Foundation grant compliance — what health systems must know
5% payout rule (IRC § 4942)

Requires private foundations to distribute at least 5% of their noncharitable-use asset FMV annually. Calculated on a 13-month rolling average. Both grants and PRIs count. Noncompliance: 30% excise tax on shortfall, escalating to 100% if uncorrected. For health systems: understand that foundations with large endowments are required to grant out capital — your NEMT proposal is helping them meet a legal obligation.

Expenditure responsibility (IRC § 4945(h))

Required when a private foundation grants to a non-public charity. Since most health systems are public charities (§ 509(a)(1)), expenditure responsibility is NOT required for standard grants. However, PRIs to for-profit subsidiaries or operating entities require a pre-grant inquiry, written agreement with repayment terms and use restrictions, and annual reporting.

Self-dealing rules (IRC § 4941)

A foundation cannot engage in certain transactions with "disqualified persons" (substantial contributors, foundation managers, certain relatives). For NEMT: if a board member of the foundation is also a board member of the health system, consult counsel before soliciting. This is the most common governance trap in hospital-foundation relationships.

Restricted vs. unrestricted grants

Foundations may restrict grants to specific purposes (e.g., "transportation coordination staff salaries for FY2026"). Restricted grants require separate accounting and reporting on use. Unrestricted grants allow the health system to apply funds to highest-need areas. For NEMT, a project-restricted grant tied to a specific outcome metric (rides provided, show rate improvement) is usually more fundable than an unrestricted operational ask.

Critical distinction — evidence requirement for Part I vs. Part II:

Community building activities (Part II) count only if the hospital can demonstrate "evidence-based health improvement outcomes."
